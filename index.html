üî¥ Priority 1: Critical Behavior Changes

NEVER pressure after customer sets a boundary

When customer said "I like to speak with my wife," the conversation should have immediately shifted to: "Absolutely, this is an important decision. I'll email you both options. Is there anything specific you'd like me to clarify before you discuss with your wife?" The pressure tactic ("waive service charge if you sign today") after this boundary was set is a serious trust violation that could lose the sale entirely.

Action: Role-play boundary recognition and respectful next steps. This is non-negotiable.

Always start with proper introduction

Every service call must begin with: "Hello, I'm [Name] from [Company]. I just completed [service] on your [system]. How are you doing today?" This establishes professionalism and credibility immediately. Starting mid-conversation ("Hey Luis, got you all done") is a compliance failure.

Action: Create opening script and practice until it's automatic.

Explicitly offer maintenance plans - don't bury them

The maintenance plan was mentioned once in the context of warranty extensions. This should be a separate, explicit offer with clear value proposition: "We also offer a maintenance program that includes [X, Y, Z] for [price]/year. Given your system's age and current mold issues, this would give you peace of mind while you decide on replacement. Would you like to enroll?"

Revenue Impact: $200-500/year recurring revenue missed on this call alone. Across all calls, this adds up to significant lost revenue.

üü¢ Priority 3: Fine-Tuning

Ask budget qualifying questions earlier

Presenting 4 detailed options is great, but asking "What budget range are you considering?" early could narrow focus faster and make conversation more efficient.

Check for comprehension more frequently

Some explanations (natural gas phase-outs, inverter technology, financing) were lengthy. Pause every 2-3 minutes to ask: "Does that make sense?" or "Any questions on that?" This ensures customer is following along.

Quantify peace-of-mind value

When discussing maintenance plans: "Our customers on maintenance plans save an average of $X per year in emergency repairs and extend their system life by Y years." Quantified benefits sell better than abstract benefits.

Confirm follow-up timeline explicitly

At closing, should have said: "I'll email both options today. When would be a good time for me to follow up - 2-3 days? A week?" This creates accountability and shows respect for customer's process.
